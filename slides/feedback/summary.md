## Overall Strengths

1. **Theory is applied, not name-dropped.** Every referenced framework (PERT, EVA, ADKAR, Wilemon & Thamhain, Schön, McClelland, Tuckman, Belbin) is used to explain a specific decision or event in the team's sprint. The PERT independence assumption violation, the ADKAR diagnosis of the C debate, and the Schön critique all demonstrate engagement with theory beyond surface labelling.

2. **Evidence is specific, timestamped, and verifiable.** Teams messages, commit logs, specific durations (45-minute pair-programming, 20-minute JSON spike, 25→12 minute stand-ups), and quantified outcomes (200% estimation error, SPI 0.75, 25% cost overrun) give the presentation credibility. Claims are supported, not asserted.

3. **Reflections are genuinely critically evaluative.** The assumption-challenging structure ensures reflections start from what was believed wrong — not what happened. The critique of Schön ("assumes I can reframe alone"), identification of power dynamics (Jamie's implicit authority), and recognition of groupthink demonstrate the highest level of reflective practice.

4. **Feedback response is documented with before/after evidence.** The shift from "topologically sorted adjacency list" to "earliest each task can start" and from descriptive to critically evaluative reflections shows genuine responsiveness to peer and tutor feedback, not just acknowledgement.

5. **PM concepts are self-applied.** The team used their own CPM tool's logic to plan their sprint (critical path through Parser → Algorithm → Integration), applied PERT to their own estimates, and used EVA to monitor their own progress. This creates a compelling meta-narrative: they learned PM by doing PM.

6. **Proactive rather than reactive teamwork.** Multiple prevention strategies (escalation rule, shared struct, JSON spike) were established before problems materialised, demonstrating strategic team management rather than firefighting.

## Overall Weaknesses

1. **Deepest analysis lives in Q&A preparation rather than presented slides.** The strongest critical analysis — stand-up contextualisation, Wilemon & Thamhain barrier applicability analysis, counter-arguments to future actions, professional transfer evidence, detailed barrier mapping (5 of 11 managed) — appears in Slide 11. The panel may never ask about it. The main presentation is strong but would be stronger if 2-3 of these insights were surfaced in Slides 2-9.

2. **Speakability exceeds allocation on Slides 6-9.** Slide 8 is 16% over word budget (435 words vs 375 allowed), and cumulative overage would likely push the presentation ~25 seconds past 10 minutes. The presentation as scripted risks overrunning unless speakers deliver faster than 150 wpm or cut material live.

3. **PERT primarily applied retrospectively.** The estimation comparison (Slide 6) shows PERT's value by comparing it against known actuals. While the Q&A prep mentions real-time PERT use during the Day 1 replan, this isn't evidenced in the main slides. Prospective use would be more compelling than retrospective validation.

4. **Leadership evolution is implicit.** The shift from transactional → transformational → servant leadership across Tuckman stages is a strong narrative but is never explicitly stated in one place. The audience must infer it from distributed references.

5. **Limited discussion of what didn't work.** Beyond the C debate and stand-up length adjustment, the presentation doesn't explore strategies that failed or were abandoned. This creates an impression of success that slightly undermines the critical evaluation claim.
