> "Week 1 taught us that iterative approaches suit 'dynamic requirements and fast-changing needs' while linear approaches suit 'stable requirements and regulated industries.' Kanban for extreme time pressure; Scrum for longer iterations with stakeholder ceremonies; hybrid when combining hardware and software."

### B. Iron Triangle — Constraint-Driven Decisions (Week 4, Page 5)

**Where to add:** First lesson — connect methodology choice to the Iron Triangle.

**Suggested script enhancement:**
> "This is fundamentally an Iron Triangle lesson. With time fixed at two days, we could not expand scope (add more features) or reduce quality (skip testing). Kanban was the methodology that let us manage within those fixed constraints by making scope flexible through the pull-based model."

### C. Estimation Methods — Bottom-Up Enabling (Week 4, Page 7)

**Where to add:** Second lesson — interface management enables estimation.

**Suggested script enhancement:**
> "The second lesson also has estimation implications. By defining clear module boundaries, we enabled bottom-up estimation — each team member could estimate their module independently. Without clear interfaces, estimation would require understanding the entire system, making it analogous estimation at best."

Reference: Week 4, Page 7 — estimation methods.

### D. Wilemon and Thamhain — "Lack of Team Member Commitment" (Week 2, Page 17)

**Where to add:** Third lesson — help-seeking as risk mitigation.

**Suggested script enhancement:**
> "Wilemon and Thamhain (1983) identify 'Lack of Team Member Commitment' as a barrier, advising leaders to 'intervene and mediate any conflict quickly.' Our third lesson extends this: it is not just about commitment problems, but about normalising help-seeking before problems escalate. A team protocol that says 'if stuck for 30 minutes, ask' prevents the commitment erosion that comes from isolation."

### E. APM on High-Performing Teams (Week 2, Page 14)

**Where to add:** Framing the lessons as what separates high-performing from average teams.

**Suggested opening:**
> "APM reports that only 10% of teams are high-performing. These three lessons represent the practices that distinguish the 10% from the 50% who are merely average — not heroic individual effort, but structural decisions that enable collective performance."

### F. Change Management — Agile Change as Continuous (Week 3, Page 19)

**Where to add:** First lesson — methodology selection.

**Suggested addition:**
> "This connects to Week 3's concept of Agile change management: 'Rather than a single, disruptive transition, change is delivered incrementally.' Our methodology choice enabled us to manage change continuously — each Kanban pull was an opportunity to inspect and adapt."

### G. Theory Y — Self-Motivated Teams (Week 2, Page 7)

**Where to add:** Third lesson — help-seeking protocol.

**Suggested addition:**
> "Normalising help-seeking aligns with Theory Y management: if individuals are naturally motivated, then asking for help is not a sign of laziness but a sign of engagement — they want to solve the problem, they just need a different perspective. Theory X would interpret help-seeking as inadequacy; Theory Y interprets it as collaboration."

### H. Contemporary Leadership — Agile Leadership (Week 2, Pages 20-21)

**Where to add:** Third lesson — distributed problem-solving.

**Suggested addition:**
> "This lesson embodies Agile leadership — 'adapting to change, fostering collaboration' (Week 2). The leader's role is not to have all answers but to create conditions where the team can unblock itself. Pair programming as risk response IS Agile leadership in action."
