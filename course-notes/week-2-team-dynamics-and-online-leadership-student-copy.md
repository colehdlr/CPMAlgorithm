Team Contract, Social Contract and 
Reward Systems
• Team contract: An agreement between you and your teammates about how 
your team will operate, a set of conventions that you plan to abide by.
• See: https://web.mit.edu/6.005/www/fa15/projects/abcplayer/team-contract/ 
• Social Contract: An agreement among team members that defines their 
commitments, expectations, and responsibilities towards each other 
and the project. It serves as a foundation for effective collaboration.
• See: https://www.belbin.com/resources/articles-directory/belbin-teams-and-the-
social-contract 
• Reward System: A method of motivating team members/stakeholders to 
elicit more effective engagement. Must consider achievement, growth and 
advancement through intrinsic (e.g. autonomy, mastery, purpose) and 
extrinsic (e.g. Salary) motivation. 
• See: https://www.apm.org.uk/blog/five-ways-to-keep-your-project-team-motivated/

Theory of Needs
• Proposed by David McClellan.
• States that people are driven by needs of achievement (e.g. 
reaching a goal), power (e.g. leading others) and affiliation (e.g. 
acceptance and belonging).

## Page 7

Theory X, Theory Y and Theory Z
• Theory X and Y were proposed by Douglas McGregor.
• Theory X: Assumes people work for the sole purpose of 
income. They are not ambitious or goal-oriented. 
• Management Style: hands-on and top-down 
approach. Examples in application include production 
or labour-intensive environments.
• Theory Y: Assumes individuals are naturally motivated. 
• Management Style: coaching and mentoring that 
encourages creativity and discussion.
• Theory Z: In Maslow's view, individuals are motivated by 
self-realisation, values and a higher calling. 
• Management Style needs to cultivate insight and 
meaning. 
• Theory Z: In Ouchi's view, the best way to motivate 
individuals is to create a job for life. 
• Management Style promotes productivity, morale and 
satisfaction.

APM on managing teams in project 
environments
• 40% of teams are dysfunctional, only 10% are high performing, and 50% are 
merely average (APM, 2022)
• Major barriers to building effective teams (Wilemon and Thamhain, 1983):
• Differing Outlooks, Priorities, Interests and Judgments of Team Members
• Role Conflicts (ambiguity in responsibilities)
• Project Objectives/Outcomes Not Clear
• Dynamic Project Environments
• Competition Over Team Leadership
• Lack of Team Definition and Structure
• Team Personnel Selection (no input from project manager in the selection process)
• Credibility of the Project Leader (poor management, poor technical judgement, lack of 
experience)
• Lack of Team Member Commitment
• Communication Problems
• Lack of Senior Management Support

## Page 15

What can we say about effective teams?
• Successful teams:
• Everyone in the team talked and listened in roughly equal measure, 
keeping contributions short and sweet.
• Members faced one another, and their conversations were energetic.
• Members connected directly with one another, not just with the team 
leader.
• Members carried on back-channel or side conversations within the 
team.
• Members periodically went exploring outside the team and brought 
information back.

## Page 16

Overcoming barriers and building effective 
teams (Wilemon and Thamhain, 1983) – Part 1
Differing Outlooks, 
Priorities, Interests, and 
Judgments of Team 
Members
• Early in the project life cycle to discover any conflicting differences. 
• Fully explain the scope of the project and the rewards which may be forthcoming upon successful project completion. 
• Sell “team” concept and explain responsibilities. 
• Try to blend individual interests with the overall project objectives.
Role Conflicts
• Early in the project, ask team members where they see themselves fitting into the project. 
• Break overall project into subsystems and subtasks (e.g., the work breakdown structure). 
• Assign/ negotiate roles. Conduct regular status review meetings to keep team informed on progress. 
• Watch for unanticipated role conflicts over the project's life.
Project Objectives/ 
Outcomes Not Clear
• Assure that all parties understand the overall project objectives. 
• Clear and frequent communication with senior management and the client. 
• Status review meetings for feedback.
Dynamic Project 
Environments
• Key project personnel must work out an agreement on the project direction and “sell” this direction to the team. 
• Educate senior management and client on the detrimental consequences of unwarranted change. 
• Forecast the “environment” within which the project will be developed. Develop contingency plans.
Competition Over Team 
Leadership
• Senior management must help establish the project manager's leadership role. 
• The project manager needs to fulfil the leadership expectations of team members. 
• Clear role and responsibility definition must be provided.
Lack of Team Definition 
and Structure
• Project leaders need to sell the team concept to senior management as well as to their team members. 
• Regular meetings with the team to reinforce the team notion as will clearly defined tasks, roles and responsibilities. 
• Visibility in memos and other forms of written media as well as senior management and client participation can unify the 
team.

## Page 17

Overcoming barriers and building effective 
teams (Wilemon and Thamhain, 1983) – Part 2
Lack of Team 
Member 
Commitment
• Determine lack of team member commitment early in the life of his project and attempt to change possible negative 
views toward the project. 
• Try to determine why insecurity exists, then work on reducing the team members' fears. 
• Project leader needs to intervene and mediate any conflict quickly. 
• If a team member's professional interests lie elsewhere, the project leader should examine ways to satisfy part of the 
team member's interests or consider replacement.
Communication 
Problems
• The project leader should devote considerable time communicating with individual team members about their needs 
and concerns. 
• The leader should provide a vehicle for timely sessions to encourage communications among the individual team 
contributors. 
• The project leader should establish regular and thorough communications with the client and senior management.
Lack of Senior 
Management 
Support
• Senior management needs to become an integral part of project reviews. 
• Project leader needs to inform management, in a timely manner, what resources are needed. 
• The project manager's relationship with senior management and ability to develop senior management support is 
critically affected by his own credibility and the visibility and priority of his project.
Project Personnel 
Selection
• Clearly discuss with potential team members the importance of the project, their role in it, what rewards might result 
upon completion, and the general “rules-of-the-road” of project management. 
• If team members remain uninterested in the project, then replacement should be considered.
Credibility of Project 
Leader
• Credibility can be enhanced by the project leaders' relationship to other key managers who support the team's efforts. 
• Fulfil leadership expectations of the team.
