## Page 11

Agile Communication Practices
Daily Stand Ups
• A 15-minute synchronisation event. Each member 
addresses: 
• What did I do? 
• What will I do? 
• Any blockers? 
• Key Insight: Promotes transparency and rapid 
impediment removal (Schwaber & Sutherland, 
2020).

## Page 12

Agile Communication Practices 
Sprint Review
• A demonstration of completed 
work to stakeholders. 
• Encourages honest feedback 
loops and ensures that the 
product vision remains aligned 
with customer expectations 
(Beck et al., 2001).

Retrospective
The team reflects on process, not product. 
What went well? 
What could improve? 
This ceremony builds psychological safety 
and cultivates a culture of open, honest 
communication (Derby & Larsen, 2006).

Change

• Change is inevitable in project environments. 
• Effective project managers must not only deliver 
outputs but also manage the human side of 
change. 
• Research consistently shows that failure to

## Page 17

ADKAR in Practice
The ADKAR model (Hiatt, 2006) is highly actionable — each element maps to a specific intervention a project manager or team leader can 
deploy. Understanding where an individual sits on the ADKAR journey enables targeted, empathetic communication.
Awareness
Why is the change needed? 
Communicate the business case clearly 
and consistently.
Desire
Motivate participation. Address concerns 
and build personal incentives to support 
change.
Knowledge
Provide training, documentation, and 
clear instructions on how to change.
Ability
Support practice and skill-building. 
Remove structural barriers to performing 
the new behaviour.
Reinforcement
Celebrate successes, provide feedback, 
and prevent regression to old habits 
(Hiatt, 2006).

## Page 18

Lewin's Model: Unfreezing the Status Quo
Why Lewin Still Matters
•
Despite its simplicity, Lewin's three-stage model 
(1951) remains one of the most widely cited 
frameworks in organisational change literature. 
•
Its strength lies in its emphasis on preparation — 
recognising that people must first be made ready 
to change before any intervention can succeed. 
•
In project management, skipping the Unfreeze 
stage is a leading cause of stakeholder resistance 
(PMI, 2013).
The Three Stages
1
Unfreeze
Challenge existing assumptions. Communicate the urgency 
and rationale for change. Build dissatisfaction with the 
current state.
2
Change
Implement new processes, behaviours, or structures. 
Provide support, guidance, and regular communication 
throughout the transition.
3
Refreeze
Embed the change into culture, policy, and practice. Reinforce 
new behaviours until they become the norm (Lewin, 1951).

## Page 19

Agile Change Management: Change as 
Continuous
•
Traditional change management treats change as a project with a start and end. Agile change management, by 
contrast, embeds adaptability into the team's DNA. 
•
Rather than a single, disruptive transition, change is delivered incrementally — each sprint offering an opportunity to 
inspect, adapt, and refine (Highsmith, 2002).
Iterative Adaptation
Short sprint cycles mean that 
change is tested in small 
increments, reducing risk and 
allowing course correction 
before issues escalate.
Stakeholder Co-Creation
Agile actively involves 
stakeholders at every review, 
ensuring that change is shaped 
with people, not imposed on them 
— a key differentiator from 
traditional models.
Continuous Feedback Loops
Ceremonies such as 
retrospectives and sprint reviews 
provide structured moments for 
teams to surface resistance, 
recalibrate, and re-commit to 
shared goals (Schwaber & 
Sutherland, 2020).
